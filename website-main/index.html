Welcome! I am an Associate Professor of Political Science at the University of Colorado at Boulder. I am also faculty affiliate at Stanford's Immigration Policy Lab (IPL) and New York University’s Center for Social Media and Politics (CSMaP), as well as a member of EGAP.

My research uses original datasets of hundreds of millions of social media posts, text and network analysis, machine learning methods, and experiments to study mass and elite political behavior in the Arab World and other comparative contexts.

I use these tools to explore drivers and mitigators of intergroup conflict and intolerance, consequences of repression, and digital dimensions of conflict—including the spread of online hate speech, extremism, and disinformation. My work has been published in the American Political Science Review, the American Journal of Political Science, World Politics, Political Communication, and the Quarterly Journal of Political Science. My work has been funded by the National Science Foundation and the Harry Frank Guggenheim Foundation.

I received my PhD in Political Science from NYU in 2018. I am a former non-resident fellow at the Brookings Institute, former Junior Fellow at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, and former CASA Fellow at the American University in Cairo. I hold a Bachelor’s in International Relations and Arabic from Tufts University.

On this website, you will find links to my CV, publications, and working papers. Please do not hesitate to contact me at alexandra.siegel@colorado.edu with any questions or to request copies of my work.

Book Project

Unsilenced: Resilience to Repression in the Digital Age

From hacking the phones of activists to doxxing and harassing journalists, governments and private actors have increasingly harnessed online tools to surveil, suppress, attack, and silence opposition. But even in the face of severe digital repression, diverse actors continue to express dissent publicly online. Relatives of arrested activists broadcast updates of prison conditions to their millions of followers and call on the international community for aid. Anonymous dissident social media accounts document government corruption and royal family dramas. Activists in exile create and disseminate viral videos calling for mobilization in their home countries. When and to what extent are Internet users resilient to digital repression? Under what circumstances can dissent still penetrate authoritarian internet control? What are the political consequences of exposure to digital repression and dissent for everyday citizens?

This book addresses the puzzle of risky online activism in the face of severe repression. Drawing on large-scale, cross-platform social media data, interviews, and experimental evidence from Egypt, Saudi Arabia, Palestine, and the US, I argue that the technological affordances of social media—including its transnational reach, anonymity, and opportunities for virality—continue to offer valuable political opportunity structures for activists and dissidents—even when they face high levels of risk. By documenting the continued resilience of online activism and its impact on everyday citizens, this book provides new insights into the evolving relationship between repression and dissent in the digital age.
